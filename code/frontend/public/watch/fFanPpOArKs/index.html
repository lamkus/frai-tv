Felix the Cat is the world's first animated pop-culture superstar, debuting as "Master Tom" in the Paramount short "Feline Follies" on 9 November 1919 and renamed Felix by his third appearance. The cartoons were produced at the Pat Sullivan Studio in Manhattan, New York: producer Pat Sullivan (an Australian-born immigrant) held the rights and marketed the character relentlessly, while lead animator Otto Messmer (American, from New Jersey) is widely credited by animation historians as Felix's true creative force, drawing well over a hundred shorts. Felix was the most popular cartoon character in the world through the mid-1920s — predating Disney's Mickey Mouse (1928) — distributed successively by Paramount/Famous Players-Lasky (1919–1921), M.J. Winkler/Winkler Pictures (1922–1925), Educational Pictures (1925–1928) and First National/Copley Pictures (1928–1930). The series declined when Sullivan resisted the switch to sound; these silent originals are now in the public domain.

Produktion / Production: Pat Sullivan Studio (Pat Sullivan Cartoon Studios), 47 West 63rd Street, Manhattan, New York City, USA. Note: Pat Sullivan was Australian-born, but the cartoons were animated in New York, USA — the production location is New York, not Australia..
